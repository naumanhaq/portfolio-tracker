The VIX (volatility index) measures market fear and uncertainty. When its 50-day average rises above longer-term averages (150/200/250-day), it signals sustained elevated fear—characteristic of bear market bottoms. High VIX during a selloff often precedes the end of panic selling.

The 150-week and 200-week moving averages act as major long-term support levels. When the S&P 500 trades within 5% of these levels during a bear market, it suggests the index is near historically significant support zones where buyers have previously stepped in. This is a mean-reversion signal.
6 — S&P 500 Weekly RSI
Weekly oversold condition		 52.6 < 35
RSI (Relative Strength Index) measures momentum on a 0-100 scale. Weekly RSI below 35 indicates the S&P 500 is oversold on a medium-term timeframe (weeks, not days). This captures sustained selling pressure that typically precedes exhaustion and reversal. Readings this low are rare and historically significant.
7 — Nasdaq Weekly RSI

The Nasdaq (tech-heavy index) weekly RSI below 35 confirms that high-growth stocks are also deeply oversold. Since tech typically leads both rallies and selloffs, extreme Nasdaq oversold conditions often signal the final stages of a bear market. When both S&P and Nasdaq RSIs are low simultaneously, it's a strong confirmation signal.
8 — S&P 500 Monthly RSI
Monthly oversold		 72.0 < 45
Monthly RSI captures the longest timeframe momentum—measured in months, not weeks or days. When monthly RSI drops below 45, it indicates a multi-month downtrend has reached oversold territory. This is a very slow-moving indicator that confirms we're in a sustained bear market, not just a correction. Monthly RSI extremes are rare and powerful.

VIX RSI above 60 on a 1-year lookback means fear has been persistently elevated for an extended period—characteristic of bear markets where waves of panic selling occur over many months. Fibonacci retracement levels identify natural support zones based on the mathematical golden ratio. The 50% retracement (halfway between the previous low and the recent high) is a psychologically significant level where markets often find support during bear markets. When the S&P retraces to this level, it suggests the market has 'given back' half of its prior gains—a common bottoming pattern. (This simplified version uses a 2-year range; ideally it would measure from the prior bear market low to the bull market peak.)

Morningstar calculates the intrinsic fair value of the entire US stock market based on discounted cash flow analysis of all publicly traded companies. When the market trades at 20% or more below this fair value estimate, it signals significant undervaluation—a fundamental (not just technical) signal. This requires manual quarterly updates from Morningstar's market fair value tool, as they don't provide a public API for this data.
